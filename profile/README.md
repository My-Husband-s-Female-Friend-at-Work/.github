# My Husband Has A Close Female Friend at Work (My Husband Talks About His Female Coworker A Lot)

There is a specific kind of unease that settles in when your husband comes home and the first story out of his mouth is about her. Again. It starts small. A funny thing she said in a meeting. How she handled a tough client. The way she always knows what to order for lunch. And somehow, without anything dramatic happening, you find yourself sitting across from your husband at dinner, feeling like an outsider in your own marriage. If that feeling sounds familiar, you are not alone, and you do not need to feel embarrassed for having it.

I often hear from wives who describe this exact situation, and what strikes me every time is how much they second-guess themselves. They wonder if they are being insecure. They ask themselves if they are making something out of nothing. But here is what I have learned after more than a decade of sitting with couples: your feelings are telling you something real, even if you are not yet sure what that something is. The goal is not to panic or shut down. The goal is to understand what is actually happening, and then respond in a way that protects both your peace and your marriage.

**Understanding Why It Bothers You So Much**

Before anything else, you have to get honest with yourself about what exactly is making you uncomfortable. Is it the frequency? The way he lights up when he mentions her name? The sense that he shares things with her that he does not share with you? These are different concerns, and they each point to something different.

Feeling unsettled when your husband talks a lot about a female coworker is not automatically a sign of jealousy or weakness. In many cases, it is a sign that something in the marriage needs attention. Maybe you feel like the emotional intimacy between you two has faded. Maybe you are craving more of his attention and enthusiasm than you are currently getting. Maybe the closeness he seems to have with this woman is filling a space you thought was yours alone.

None of that makes you irrational. It makes you human. The problem comes when we mistake the feeling for a verdict. Feeling worried does not mean he is doing something wrong. But it also does not mean you should ignore what you are feeling. Sit with it. Ask yourself what specifically is bothering you. That clarity will help you more than any argument ever could.

**The Difference Between a Harmless Friendship and a Threat to Your Marriage**

Not every close friendship your husband has with a woman is a red flag. People spend a third of their lives at work. Friendships form. Inside jokes develop. That is normal. But there is a difference between a healthy work friendship and something that starts to pull him away from you emotionally.

A harmless friendship looks like this: he mentions her occasionally, maybe works on projects with her, and has a collegial respect for her. He is transparent about who she is and has no reason to hide the friendship from you. He still comes home present, engaged, and interested in you.

Something worth paying closer attention to looks different. He brings her up constantly, often in ways that feel almost involuntary, like he cannot help himself. He gets defensive when you ask even simple questions. He starts comparing you to her, even subtly. He seems more excited about going to work than he used to be. He shares things with her, then forgets to share them with you. These patterns do not confirm that something inappropriate is happening, but they do suggest that the friendship has taken on an emotional weight that deserves a conversation.

**How to Talk to Your Husband Without It Turning Into a Fight**

This is the part most wives dread, and honestly, it is the part that matters most. How you bring this up will determine whether the conversation brings you closer or pushes you apart.

Do not lead with accusations. Do not open with "I think you have feelings for her" or "You talk about her too much." Even if those things feel true, starting there puts him on defense before he even has a chance to hear you. When people feel attacked, they stop listening. They start protecting themselves. And nothing gets resolved.

Instead, start with how you feel. Use your own experience as the entry point. Something like: "I have been feeling a little disconnected from you lately, and I have noticed I feel a pang when you talk about her a lot. I am not accusing you of anything. I just wanted to be honest with you about how I have been feeling." That framing is disarming. It is honest without being combative. It invites him in rather than putting him on trial.

Then give him space to respond. Really listen. Do not use his response as ammunition. Try to understand what this friendship means to him, and share clearly what you need from him in return.

**What You Actually Need Is Emotional Closeness With Him**

Here is the part that often gets lost in these conversations: the problem is rarely just about her. She is the visible piece, but the deeper issue is usually about what is happening between the two of you.

When a husband feels closely connected to his wife, when he is coming home to warmth, conversation, real intimacy, and genuine friendship, outside friendships rarely shake the foundation of a marriage. The risk goes up when the marriage has become more functional than close. When the two of you are living parallel lives, handling logistics, raising kids, paying bills, but not really connecting. That kind of emotional distance creates openings, sometimes without anyone even realizing it.

So while it is fair to talk to your husband about how his friendship is affecting you, also ask yourself what the state of your emotional connection has been lately. When did you two last have a real conversation? When did you last laugh together? When did you last tell each other something vulnerable? These questions matter. Rebuilding closeness with your husband is not a distraction from the issue. It is the issue.

**Setting Boundaries That Actually Protect Your Marriage**

Boundaries in marriage are not about control. You cannot control who your husband talks to at work, and trying to will likely backfire. What you can do is have an honest conversation about what feels okay and what does not, and ask him to take your feelings seriously.

For example, it is reasonable to ask that he not text her late at night. It is reasonable to expect that he does not share deeply personal things about your marriage with her. It is reasonable to ask that if you are not comfortable with a particular dynamic, he respects that and takes it seriously rather than dismissing it.

What is not fair is issuing ultimatums rooted in fear, demanding he end every friendship with a woman, or monitoring his phone. That approach breeds resentment and erodes trust in a different way.

The boundaries that work are ones you both agree on, ones that come from a place of protecting the marriage rather than policing each other. Have that conversation. Be clear about what you need. And listen to what he needs too.

**What to Do If Your Husband Dismisses Your Concerns**

This part is important. If you bring your feelings to your husband with care and honesty, and he brushes you off, tells you that you are being crazy, or refuses to take your concerns seriously, that is information. Not about the female coworker. About him.

A husband who respects you will not make you feel foolish for being honest about your feelings. He may disagree. He may see things differently. But he will listen, and he will care that you are hurting, even if he does not think he has done anything wrong.

If he consistently invalidates your feelings or makes you feel like the problem every time you try to talk about something that bothers you, that is a pattern worth examining. Not because it means he is unfaithful, but because emotional dismissal in a marriage is its own kind of problem. You deserve to be heard. A marriage where one person's feelings are treated as an inconvenience is not a healthy one, and that matters regardless of what is or is not happening with any coworker.

At the end of the day, what you are really asking for is not for your husband to have fewer friends. You are asking to feel like you are still his person. That is not an unreasonable thing to want. It is one of the most fundamental things a marriage is supposed to provide. Do not shrink that desire. Name it, own it, and go after it together.
